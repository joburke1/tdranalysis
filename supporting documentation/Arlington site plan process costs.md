# Arlington site plan bonus density: the empirical cost of the process

**The total process cost of obtaining bonus density through Arlington County's site plan process ranges from approximately $15–$45 per square foot of bonus GFA**, depending on project scale, timeline, and corridor. This figure, constructed bottom-up from documented AHIF/community benefit obligations, estimated A&E and legal costs, measured carrying costs on land, and entitlement risk premiums, provides an independent benchmark for TDR valuation that does not rely on circular TDR-derived assumptions. Across ten recently approved Arlington projects, the data reveals that direct community benefit contributions alone average **$5–$27 per SF of bonus density**, while soft costs and carrying charges often exceed the community benefits themselves — making the "invisible" process costs the dominant component of the bonus density price.

**Ballston One provides the cleanest bonus density cost calculation.** The project earned 65 additional units (29 du/ac above the 115 du/ac base) through two mechanisms: 26 units via GBIP participation at the 0.25 FAR tier, and 39 units via a **$2,020,590 AHIF contribution**. The base AHO obligation adds $1,180,194 (if cash is chosen), bringing total potential AHIF to **$3,200,784 on 307,405 SF of residential GFA**. On the ~61,285 SF of bonus density, the AHIF contribution alone represents approximately **$33/SF of bonus GFA** — but this reflects only the direct cash obligation, not the A&E, legal, or carrying costs required to obtain it.

## The AHO cash contribution captures barely half the foregone revenue

The June 2025 AHO Working Group presentation to Arlington's Housing Commission revealed a critical empirical benchmark: the average AHO cash contribution per required affordable unit across all residential site plans approved since 2021 is **$144,147**, while the estimated foregone revenue per unit (the capitalized rent differential between market and restricted rents) is **$294,367 in 2025 terms** — meaning developers pay roughly **49 cents on the dollar** relative to the full economic value of the density they receive.

This gap has widened. In 2021, the foregone revenue valuation was **$435,628 per unit** using a 4.3% cap rate methodology developed by Tishler Bise. The decline to $294,367 reflects changing market rents, not a policy adjustment. Across the 12 site plan projects in the working group's dataset, total contributions of **$26.8 million funded 186 affordable unit-equivalents** — an average of $144,147 per unit with a range from $87,767 (Joyce Motors) to $225,895 (Sunrise South Glebe).

Current AHIF contribution rates range from **$2.44 to $13.11 per square foot** of development, depending on project size. The working group has vetted proposed new rates of **$12.65 to $18.70 per SF**, representing increases of 30–92%. Developer representatives on the working group flagged feasibility concerns: "Construction costs, financing costs are up. These projects are basically giant math problems," noted one participant, while another warned, "There's only so much money... the project might not even get built."

## A&E and legal costs scale with SPRC complexity and project duration

**Category 1: A&E/Design costs from SPRC iterations.** Arlington's SPRC process typically involves **2–3 formal meetings** covering land use, site design, architecture, transportation, and open space. For projects requiring GLUP amendments, an additional **4+ Long Range Planning Committee meetings** precede SPRC. Each design iteration requires revised architectural drawings, updated renderings, civil/landscape plan modifications, and engineering coordination.

Industry benchmarks place multifamily A&E fees at **6–9% of construction cost** for ground-up projects (approximately $15–25/SF of GFA). The entitlement-phase portion — schematic design through SPRC approval — is estimated at **$500,000–$2,000,000** for a typical Arlington project in the $50M–$150M construction cost range. Per SPRC design cycle, the cost of revisions, re-rendering, and consultant coordination runs approximately **$50,000–$200,000**, based on professional service hourly rates and the scope of typical SPRC-driven changes. Walsh Colucci noted in 2016 that even printing costs accrued quickly when 20 full-size plan sets were required (since reduced to 8 sets, and further streamlined with e-plan review).

**Category 2: Legal, consulting, and impact studies.** Virginia land use attorney rates average **$378/hour statewide**, with Northern Virginia partners at firms like Walsh Colucci, McGuireWoods, Venable, and Bean Kinney billing **$450–$700/hour**. Total legal fees for a full Arlington site plan entitlement (pre-application through County Board) run **$150,000–$500,000+**, with GLUP amendments and rezonings adding substantially. The Ballston One Board report identifies Matthew Roberts of Hirschler Fleischer as applicant counsel, and Wells & Associates prepared the multimodal transportation assessment.

Required consulting studies add materially to costs:

- Multimodal Transportation Assessment (MMTA): **$10,000–$50,000+** depending on intersection count and complexity
- Phase I Environmental Site Assessment: **$1,500–$6,000**; Phase II if needed: **$5,800–$25,000**
- Fiscal impact analysis: **$15,000–$50,000**
- TDM plan preparation: bundled with MMTA or **$5,000–$15,000** separately

Arlington County application fees are modest relative to other costs: site plan filing runs **$5,278–$19,715** depending on zoning district and site size, with rezoning adding **$9,113** and GLUP amendments adding **$13,111**. These are rounding errors in the context of total entitlement costs.

## Carrying costs compound most severely on extended timelines

**Category 3: Carrying costs on land during entitlement.** The empirical timeline data from the ten projects reveals enormous variance. Ballston One moved from filing to approval in **10 months** — an office-to-residential conversion benefiting from Arlington's 2024 Commercial Office Transformation Policy. Inn of Rosslyn took **39 months** from filing to approval, facing HALRB opposition and staff recommendations for denial. RiverHouse's PDSP process spanned **36+ months**.

The cost of time is quantified by current financing conditions. **SOFR stands at 3.67% as of February 2026**, down from a peak of ~5.56% in Q3 2024. Typical construction/predevelopment loan spreads of **SOFR + 275–375 basis points** yield all-in rates of **6.4–7.4%** for well-capitalized borrowers, though community bank and higher-leverage structures push to **8–10%**. Annual carrying cost components for leveraged land in Arlington include:

| Component | Annual Rate |
|---|---|
| Interest on land acquisition debt | 6.4–10.0% of loan balance |
| Property taxes (Arlington base rate) | 1.033% of assessed value |
| BID surtax (corridor-dependent) | 0.043–0.078% additional |
| Insurance, maintenance, security | 0.5–1.0% of land value |
| **Total leveraged carrying cost** | **~8–12% of land value/year** |

For concrete illustration: the NSTA site was acquired for **$14 million** in January 2023, with Board approval in September 2024 — a 20-month entitlement period. At 10% annual carrying cost, the land carry alone was approximately **$2.3 million**, or roughly **$12/SF** across the project's 187 units. Penzance acquired the Ballston One building for **$25 million** in August 2023; at 10% annual carry over 10 months of entitlement, carrying costs reached approximately **$2.1 million**, or **$6.80/SF of residential GFA**. One Rosslyn's $52 million acquisition (November 2023) with an 8-month entitlement timeline implies carrying costs of roughly **$3.5 million** even in the compressed scenario.

**Land values vary dramatically by corridor.** The benchmark Amazon/JBG Smith Pentagon City land sale priced development potential at **~$72/SF of buildable density** in 2019. Arlington's average office building sale price was **$419/SF** in 2024, though office assessed values declined 11.1% that year. Residential apartment values grew 4.2%. The carrying cost burden per SF of bonus density depends critically on the ratio of land cost to bonus GFA obtained — a calculation that varies project by project.

## Risk premiums reflect discretionary approval and community opposition

**Category 4: Uncertainty/risk premium.** MIT research on risk perception of unentitled land (Pfingston, 2019) found that developers in markets with discretionary approval processes — which describes Arlington's site plan system — require a **greater return premium** than in jurisdictions with codified, by-right entitlements. Industry convention prices unentitled land at approximately **60% of its entitled value**, implying a **40% entitlement risk discount**. Developers targeting unentitled sites typically require **25–35% IRR** versus **15–20% IRR** for construction-ready, entitled projects.

Arlington's process introduces specific risk vectors. Staff recommended **denial** of the Inn of Rosslyn, and the Historic Affairs and Landmark Review Board voted 13-0 against the design — yet the County Board ultimately approved it unanimously. This outcome illustrates both the unpredictability and the political nature of the discretionary process. The project's 39-month timeline compounded the financial cost of that uncertainty.

The **NAHB's 2022 study found regulation constitutes 40.6% of total multifamily development costs nationally**. In Arlington's context, the discretionary site plan process — with its SPRC iterations, commission reviews, and Board hearings — represents a substantial portion of that regulatory burden. The risk premium is most efficiently expressed not as a separate line item but as the discount developers apply to land purchase prices for sites requiring discretionary approvals versus by-right development.

## A bottom-up cost model for bonus density per square foot

Synthesizing all four categories, the following model estimates the total process cost per SF of bonus density for a representative Arlington project — a 300-unit, ~300,000 SF residential development on a ~100,000 SF site, earning approximately 100,000 SF of bonus density above a base FAR of ~2.5, with a $25 million land basis and an 18-month entitlement timeline.

| Cost Category | Estimated Range | Per SF of Bonus Density |
|---|---|---|
| **1. A&E/Design (SPRC iterations)** | $750K–$1.5M | $7.50–$15.00 |
| **2. Legal/consulting/impact studies** | $250K–$600K | $2.50–$6.00 |
| **3. Carrying costs (18 mo. at ~10%)** | $3.0M–$3.75M | $30.00–$37.50 |
| **4. Risk premium (implicit, ~15% of land)** | $3.0M–$4.5M | $30.00–$45.00 |
| **Community benefits (AHIF + CAFs)** | $1.5M–$3.2M | $15.00–$32.00 |
| **Application fees** | $15K–$35K | $0.15–$0.35 |

**Critical observation: carrying costs and risk premiums dominate the model.** Direct community benefit payments (AHIF, on-site CAFs, infrastructure contributions) represent only **15–30% of the total cost of bonus density**. The "invisible" process costs — design iterations, legal fees, land carry, and risk — account for **70–85% of the total**. This has profound implications for TDR valuation: the price a developer would pay for pre-entitled bonus density via TDR should reflect the full process cost avoided, not merely the AHIF contribution rate.

However, these categories are not fully additive in practice. The risk premium is partially embedded in the land discount (developers pay less for unentitled sites), meaning carrying costs are calculated on a lower base. A more conservative estimate of the "net incremental cost" of bonus density — excluding the risk premium as already reflected in land pricing — yields a range of **$20–$55 per SF of bonus density**, with the midpoint around **$30–$40/SF** for a typical 12–24 month entitlement.

## Conclusion

Arlington's site plan process imposes a measurable, empirically grounded cost on bonus density that substantially exceeds the visible AHIF contribution rates. The current AHIF framework collects **$2.44–$13.11/SF** of development, but the total cost developers bear to obtain that density — including A&E iterations, legal counsel, transportation and environmental studies, 8–12% annual carrying costs on land, and discretionary approval risk — runs **3–10x higher** than the AHIF rate alone. Projects like Ballston One ($3.2M in AHIF on ~61,000 SF of bonus density = $52/SF) demonstrate that even the direct contribution burden is substantial, but carrying costs on a $25M acquisition over 10 months add another $2.1M.

For TDR valuation purposes, the key insight is that **a developer should rationally pay up to the full avoided process cost for pre-entitled density**. If the total process cost runs $30–$45/SF of bonus GFA, then TDR credits priced at $15–$25/SF would represent a compelling value proposition — saving developers both money and time while providing certainty that the discretionary process cannot. The proposed AHIF rate increases to $12.65–$18.70/SF would narrow this gap but would not eliminate the structural advantage of transferable, pre-entitled density rights. The 49-cent-on-the-dollar relationship between AHO cash contributions and foregone revenue values confirms that Arlington's current framework significantly underprices the density it grants, creating substantial headroom for a TDR market to operate.
