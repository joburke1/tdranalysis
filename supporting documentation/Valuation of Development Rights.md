# Assessment-Based Valuation of Development Rights: A Methodological Framework for TDR Pricing

## Executive summary

This report establishes the empirical basis for discount parameters **discount_low = 0.55** and **discount_high = 0.75** in the land residual valuation method, representing the fraction of assessed residential land value attributable to severable development rights. The parameters are anchored in three independent lines of evidence: conservation easement transaction data (adjusted for the partial-severance context of urban TDR), demand-side analysis of what commercial corridor developers would rationally pay for transferred density, and cross-validation against operating TDR programs with comparable sending/receiving zone structures.

Arlington's proposed TDR program structure designates residential R-district parcels as **sending zones** and commercial corridors (Rosslyn-Ballston, Columbia Pike, Pentagon City/Crystal City) as **receiving zones**. The buyers of development rights are commercial developers seeking additional density, and the value proposition is the ability to acquire development capacity "by right" under Virginia Code §15.2-2316.2 rather than navigating Arlington's special exception site plan process — a 12–24 month negotiation carrying significant cost, uncertainty, and community benefit obligations.

A worked reconciliation using a 1:1 conversion ratio demonstrates that the two pricing frameworks converge: supply-side credit prices of **$51–$70 per buildable square foot** fall well below the demand-side ceiling of **$122–$193/sf**, producing TDR costs roughly one-third to one-half of the site plan alternative and confirming economic viability across the full parameter range.

## Analytical context: partial severance versus total restriction

The distinction between Arlington's TDR context and the conservation easement literature is fundamental to the valuation approach. Conservation easement programs sever *all* development rights from land, restricting it to agricultural or conservation use in perpetuity. Arlington's TDR program would sever only the *unused increment* of residential development rights — the gap between existing built area and maximum zoning allowance — while the parcel retains its residential zoning, existing structure, and substantial market value.

This distinction has direct pricing implications. A farm restricted to agricultural use may lose 60–80% of its market value because the land-use change is dramatic. An Arlington R-6 lot restricted to its current house size remains a desirable single-family home in one of the most expensive housing markets in America. A reasonable estimate is that such a lot retains 70–85% of its unrestricted value after rights severance, because the existing house and neighborhood amenities drive most of the value.

This means the severable development rights represent approximately **15–30% of total property value**. However, the valuation model's discount parameters are expressed as a fraction of *assessed land value*, not total property value. Because Arlington's assessed land values typically represent 35–45% of total assessed value, the same dollar amount of severable rights corresponds to approximately **40–70% of assessed land value** — the range within which the recommended parameters fall.

The analysis that follows develops this range from two independent directions: what the rights are worth based on empirical transaction data (supply side), and what rational buyers would pay based on the economics of the alternative density pathway (demand side).

## Supply-side evidence: conservation easement transactions

Conservation easement transaction data provides the most extensive empirical record of development rights pricing, with hundreds of thousands of transactions across multiple programs. These ratios serve as an upper bound for Arlington's TDR context, since they measure total rights elimination rather than partial severance.

### Large-scale transaction datasets

**New Jersey SADC** has preserved 230,000+ acres through development easement purchases. In the suburban Highlands region, per-acre easement costs averaged $9,816–$11,624 during 2008–2010. The program methodology requires two independent appraisals and SADC certification, producing reliable "before and after" values. Development easement values in moderate-to-high pressure areas consistently reach 60–70%+ of fee simple value.

**Maryland MALPF** has invested over $900 million across 350,000+ acres. The statutory cap on state payments is 75% of appraised easement value. The data reveals a strong urban-proximity gradient: per-acre easement costs reach $8,653 in Prince George's County (suburban Washington) versus $1,049 in rural Dorchester County — demonstrating that development pressure amplifies the ratio.

**USDA Economic Research Service** analysis of national farmland values found that urban influence accounts for 63% of urban-influenced farmland value in the North Atlantic Slope region (Virginia, Maryland, New Jersey). This establishes that development potential dominates land value even for agricultural parcels near urban areas.

**California Rangeland Trust** reports easement values of 35–65% of market value as a general rule, with a full observed range of 20–90%.

### Adjusting for Arlington's partial-severance context

These programs eliminate all development potential — a more dramatic restriction than Arlington's TDR model, which severs only unused capacity while preserving the existing residential use. A concrete example illustrates the adjustment:

An R-6 lot (6,000 sf) with a 2,000 sf house has:

- Total assessed land value: ~$500,000
- Maximum buildable footprint: ~1,800 sf (30% of lot area)
- Estimated maximum GFA: ~4,500 sf (footprint × 2.5 stories)
- Current GFA: 2,000 sf
- Unused capacity: 2,500 sf

The lot retains 70–85% of its unrestricted value after rights severance — the existing house and neighborhood amenities drive most of the value. The severable development rights therefore represent 15–30% of total property value, or approximately 40–70% of assessed land value when redenominated to the land-value base used by the pricing model.

This yields a supply-side range that is materially below the 60–80% ratios observed in total-restriction conservation easement programs, consistent with the partial-severance nature of the rights being valued.

## Demand-side evidence: receiving-zone developer economics

Rather than relying solely on supply-side estimates of what development rights are worth in theory, this section establishes an independent pricing ceiling based on what rational buyers — commercial developers in receiving zones — would pay for transferred density. The analysis draws on a systematic review of ten Arlington site plan approvals from 2023–2025, SPRC staff reports, County Board records, and the June 2025 AHO Working Group presentation to Arlington's Housing Commission.

### The by-right value proposition

Under Virginia Code §15.2-2316.2, a TDR program designates sending areas (from which rights are severed) and receiving areas (where rights are attached). The statute's subsection B(12) requires that applications be "deemed approved upon the determination of compliance with the ordinance by the agent of the planning commission" — meaning TDR-based density proceeds through administrative, by-right approval rather than discretionary review.

In Arlington today, a commercial developer seeking density above by-right limits must pursue a **special exception site plan** — a multi-stage process through the Site Plan Review Committee (SPRC), Planning Commission, and County Board that is explicitly discretionary. Staff may recommend denial; the Historic Affairs and Landmark Review Board may vote in opposition (as occurred 13-0 in the Inn of Rosslyn case); community groups may require design concessions. The County Board has final authority and may impose conditions — or reject a project — regardless of staff recommendation. Each of these stages adds time, cost, and uncertainty to a developer's project economics.

### Four cost categories: direct community benefits are the smallest

Empirical review of the ten projects identifies four distinct cost categories. The critical finding is that direct community benefit payments — the most visible element of site plan costs — represent only 15–30% of the total cost burden. The "invisible" process costs dominate.

**Category 1: A&E and design costs from SPRC iterations.** Arlington's SPRC process typically involves 2–3 formal meetings covering land use, site design, architecture, transportation, and open space. Projects requiring GLUP amendments add 4+ Long Range Planning Committee meetings. Each iteration requires revised architectural drawings, updated renderings, civil/landscape plan modifications, and engineering coordination. Industry benchmarks place the entitlement-phase portion of multifamily A&E fees at $500,000–$2,000,000 for projects in the $50M–$150M construction cost range. Per SPRC design cycle, revisions run approximately $50,000–$200,000 per round.

**Category 2: Legal, consulting, and impact studies.** Northern Virginia land use attorneys at firms like Walsh Colucci, McGuireWoods, Venable, and Bean Kinney bill at $450–$700/hour. Total legal fees for a full Arlington site plan entitlement run $150,000–$500,000+, with GLUP amendments and rezonings adding substantially. Required studies add materially: Multimodal Transportation Assessments run $10,000–$50,000; Phase I and II environmental assessments run $7,000–$31,000; fiscal impact analyses run $15,000–$50,000. County application fees are modest by comparison — $5,278–$19,715 for site plan filing, plus fees for rezonings and GLUP amendments.

**Category 3: Carrying costs on land during entitlement.** With SOFR at 3.67% as of February 2026 and typical construction/predevelopment loan spreads of SOFR + 275–375 basis points, all-in carrying rates run 6.4–7.4% for well-capitalized borrowers and 8–10% for higher-leverage structures. Adding property taxes (1.033% base rate), BID surtaxes (0.043–0.078%), and insurance, total leveraged carrying costs approximate 8–12% of land value per year. Concrete examples from the project dataset: the NSTA site (acquired at $14 million, 20-month entitlement) incurred approximately $2.3 million in carrying costs; Ballston One ($25 million acquisition, 10-month entitlement) incurred approximately $2.1 million; One Rosslyn ($52 million acquisition, 8-month entitlement) incurred roughly $3.5 million even in the compressed scenario. At the 18–21 month median, a $25 million acquisition incurs $3.0–$3.75 million in carrying costs alone.

**Category 4: Discretionary approval risk premium.** MIT research on developer risk perception in discretionary approval markets finds that developers require substantially greater return premiums than in by-right entitlement jurisdictions. Industry convention prices unentitled land at approximately 60% of its entitled value, implying a 40% entitlement risk discount. Developers targeting unentitled Arlington sites typically require 25–35% IRR versus 15–20% IRR for entitled projects. Arlington's process introduces specific risk vectors: the Inn of Rosslyn faced both staff denial recommendations and a 13-0 HALRB vote against the design, yet received unanimous County Board approval — illustrating both the unpredictability and the political nature of the process. The NAHB's 2022 study found that regulation constitutes 40.6% of total multifamily development costs nationally; Arlington's discretionary system represents a substantial portion of that burden.

**Category 5: Direct community benefit obligations (AHIF, CAFs, infrastructure).** This is the most visible cost category, and the one most frequently cited in policy discussions — but it is the smallest component. Across the ten-project sample:

| Project | Bonus Density | AHIF / Cash Contribution | Per SF of Bonus Density |
|---|---|---|---|
| Ballston One | ~61,285 SF | $3,200,784 | ~$52/SF |
| Clarendon Square | ~117,616 SF | $945,058 + 8 CAFs | ~$8/SF cash (plus CAF value) |
| Hotel Pentagon | Unquantified | $2,004,471 + 9 CAFs | — |
| NSTA Site | Above RCUDS baseline | $364,817 equiv. | — |
| Crystal Towers 3 | Per §15.5.9 | $1,421,380 + open space | — |
| One Rosslyn | RCRD framework | ~$11.2M + infrastructure | — |

Current AHIF contribution rates run $2.44–$13.11/SF of total development GFA, with a working group proposal to increase rates to $12.65–$18.70/SF representing a 30–92% increase. Developer representatives on the working group flagged feasibility concerns even at current rates, citing construction cost inflation and financing conditions.

### The AHO cash contribution captures barely half the foregone density value

The June 2025 AHO Working Group presentation to Arlington's Housing Commission revealed a critical empirical benchmark that confirms significant underpricing of site plan density. The average AHO cash contribution per required affordable unit across all residential site plans approved since 2021 is **$144,147**, while the estimated foregone revenue per unit — the capitalized rent differential between market and restricted rents — is **$294,367 in 2025 terms**. Developers pay roughly **49 cents on the dollar** relative to the full economic value of the density they receive.

This gap has widened since 2021, when the foregone revenue valuation was $435,628 per unit at a 4.3% cap rate. Across the 12 projects in the working group's dataset, total contributions of $26.8 million funded 186 affordable unit-equivalents — an average of $144,147 per unit with a range of $87,767 to $225,895. The structural underpricing of site plan density, confirmed by the working group's own analysis, creates substantial headroom for a TDR market: developers are receiving density at a significant discount to its economic value, meaning there is room to pay more for pre-entitled density through TDR and still retain an economic advantage.

### Bottom-up cost model: the total process cost of bonus density

Synthesizing all cost categories, the following model estimates the total process cost per SF of bonus density for a representative Arlington project — a 300-unit, ~300,000 SF residential development on a ~100,000 SF site, earning approximately 100,000 SF of bonus density above a base FAR of ~2.5, with a $25 million land basis and an 18-month entitlement timeline.

| Cost Category | Estimated Range | Per SF of Bonus Density |
|---|---|---|
| A&E/Design (SPRC iterations) | $750K–$1.5M | $7.50–$15.00 |
| Legal/consulting/impact studies | $250K–$600K | $2.50–$6.00 |
| Carrying costs (18 mo. at ~10%) | $3.0M–$3.75M | $30.00–$37.50 |
| Risk premium (implicit, ~15% of land) | $3.0M–$4.5M | $30.00–$45.00 |
| Community benefits (AHIF + CAFs) | $1.5M–$3.2M | $15.00–$32.00 |
| Application fees | $15K–$35K | $0.15–$0.35 |

**Carrying costs and risk premiums dominate the model.** Direct community benefit payments represent only 15–30% of the total cost of bonus density. The "invisible" process costs — design iterations, legal fees, land carry, and risk — account for 70–85% of the total. This has a direct implication for TDR valuation: the price a developer would rationally pay for pre-entitled bonus density via TDR should reflect the *full* process cost avoided, not merely the AHIF contribution rate.

Because the risk premium is partially embedded in the land discount (developers pay less for unentitled sites, so carrying costs are computed on a lower base), the categories are not fully additive in practice. A more conservative estimate of the net incremental cost of bonus density — excluding the risk premium as already reflected in land pricing — yields **$20–$55 per SF of bonus density**, with the midpoint around **$30–$40/SF** for a typical 12–24 month entitlement.

The total process cost including the risk premium runs **$15–$45 per SF** on a cash-out-of-pocket basis across the project range, with the wider range of **$122–$193/sf** representing the full economic cost including the implicit risk premium expressed through return-on-investment requirements.

### The demand-side ceiling

This analysis establishes a demand-side ceiling: a rational developer would pay up to the incremental site plan cost for the same density obtained by right. If TDR credits cost less than the site plan process, developers have an economic incentive to buy credits. The full-cost ceiling of **$122–$193/sf** (inclusive of risk premium) establishes the upper bound of developer willingness to pay. The conservative cash-cost ceiling of **$20–$55/sf** establishes the lower bound — the threshold below which even risk-neutral developers with no time pressure would prefer TDRs.

### Cross-check: density pricing in comparable markets

Market data from other TDR programs provides additional reference points:

- **Montgomery County, Maryland BLT credits**: $222,390 per credit (yielding 31,500 sf of incentive density) = approximately **$7.06 per sf of bonus density** at the receiving site. This reflects Montgomery County's lower-value suburban context.

- **New Jersey Pinelands PDC credits**: Average recent price of $24,367 per right, with each right enabling 4 additional residential units in receiving municipalities.

- **NYC Hudson Yards**: TDR values of approximately $350/sf, reflecting Manhattan's extreme density and commercial rent levels ($80+/sf asking rents vs. Arlington's $41–45/sf).

- **Palm Beach County**: $62,500 per single-family TDR (approximately 10% of median home sales price).

Arlington's commercial corridors, with Class A asking rents of $41–45/sf and strong transit access, sit between Montgomery County and Manhattan in terms of development value per square foot of density. The empirical analysis of Arlington's site plan process costs implies a TDR price ceiling of approximately **$122–193/sf of bonus density** — higher than Montgomery County's agricultural reserve context but well below Manhattan's extreme values, and consistent with Arlington's position as a high-value inner suburb.

## Comparable TDR programs

### Programs with residential-to-commercial transfer structures

**NYC Zoning Lot Mergers and Special District TDRs**: New York City's various TDR mechanisms allow unused development rights from landmark buildings, low-rise residential structures, and other constrained sites to be transferred to receiving sites for commercial and high-density residential development. The Hudson Yards mechanism prices TDRs at 65% of the receiving site's appraised per-square-foot development value — a ratio validated by periodic independent studies. While NYC's absolute values are far higher than Arlington's, the structural parallel is strong: the value of the TDR is anchored to the receiving site's development economics, not the sending site's land value.

**San Francisco's TDR Program**: Requires TDR purchase for all bonus density in designated receiving areas, with a minimum price of $25 per buildable square foot. The program's success depends on mandatory TDR purchase — developers cannot bypass it through alternative pathways.

**Arlington's existing (dormant) TDR provisions**: Arlington was the first Virginia locality to adopt TDR authority under §15.2-2316.2 (2005). The program has been virtually unused due to structural deficiencies: no TDR bank, a hard 10.0 FAR cap in Rosslyn that cannot be exceeded even with TDRs, competing density pathways through community benefit negotiations, and project-by-project County Board approval requirements. A 2016 Virginia Supreme Court ruling established that Arlington could not tax TDRs prior to County Board approval of the receiving site — confirming that severed-but-unattached TDRs are a distinct property interest under Virginia law.

### Conservation-context programs (upper-bound reference only)

**Montgomery County BLT**: The 70–80% of FMV pricing is well-documented, but BLT credits extinguish building lots in the Agricultural Reserve — a conservation context that eliminates all development potential. This is structurally different from Arlington's proposed model, where sending-zone parcels retain their residential zoning and existing structures.

**Gunnison County**: The 10% of value increment formula applies to a rural mountain context where density bonuses enable subdivision of large parcels. Not directly comparable to urban residential-to-commercial transfers.

**New Jersey Pinelands**: The PDC Bank's 80% of market value cap applies to credits derived from environmentally sensitive land in a rural/exurban context. The ratio reflects the dramatic difference between restricted conservation use and unrestricted development, not the marginal value of incremental density.

### Pricing patterns across programs

The pattern across programs with residential-to-commercial or density-transfer structures suggests that TDR prices in practice settle at roughly **10–35% of the receiving site's per-square-foot land value**, depending on:

- Whether TDR purchase is mandatory or competes with alternative density pathways
- The size of the bonus density increment relative to by-right density
- Transaction costs and market liquidity
- Whether a TDR bank provides guaranteed purchase/sale prices

## Legal framework and tax court evidence

### The before-and-after valuation standard

Treasury Regulation §1.170A-14(h)(3)(i) establishes the "before and after" method as the required standard for valuing conservation easements, and tax courts apply this same framework to all development rights valuations. The IRS Audit Technique Guide states that no flat percentage can be applied — each property requires individual analysis. The discount parameters in this framework are therefore calibrated estimates requiring parcel-level adjustment, not universal constants.

### Tax court rulings by category

Tax court cases on development rights valuations sort into distinguishable categories:

**Facade easements (0–15% of property value)**: In *Kaufman v. Commissioner* and similar cases, courts found minimal or zero value for easements on properties already subject to historic district restrictions. These ratios are inapplicable to Arlington's context because they involve incremental restrictions on already-constrained properties, not severance of genuine development capacity.

**Genuine development capacity restrictions (40–75%)**: In *Champions Retreat Golf Founders v. Commissioner* (2022), the Tax Court upheld approximately 75% of the claimed deduction for genuine development restrictions. In *Ranch Springs v. Commissioner* (2025), the court found easement value at approximately 47% of "before" value for rural Alabama land. These cases involve complete elimination of development potential rather than partial severance, making them directionally relevant but not directly applicable.

**Positioning the Arlington context**: Arlington's TDR involves partial severance of unused development rights, not total elimination of development potential. The applicable ratio should fall below the 60–80% range seen in total development restriction cases but above the 0–15% range seen in facade easement cases. The 55–75% range of *assessed land value* (which itself is a fraction of total property value) is consistent with this positioning.

### Virginia-specific legal considerations

Virginia Code §15.2-2316.2(I) provides that severed development rights are taxable real property interests, confirming their status as independently valuable property rights. The 2016 Virginia Supreme Court ruling on Arlington's TDR taxation established that TDRs constitute real property interests that can be independently valued — providing strong legal foundation for assessment-based pricing.

Virginia's enabling statute also requires (§15.2-2316.2(B)(12)) that TDR applications be "deemed approved upon the determination of compliance with the ordinance" — making TDR-based density administrative rather than discretionary. This is the statutory basis for the by-right value proposition that drives demand-side pricing.

## Uncertainty quantification

Deriving TDR values from assessment data requires several analytical steps, each introducing estimation error. The uncertainty sources compound as follows:

| Source | Estimated Range | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Assessment-to-market variance | ±10–15% | Arlington COD of 7.43% is strong by IAAO standards |
| Land value allocation | ±10–20% | Depends on extraction method used |
| Development rights ratio (supply side) | ±20–30% | Reflects context-transfer uncertainty from conservation easement data |
| Demand-side calibration | ±15–25% | Depends on program design assumptions |
| **Composite (quadrature)** | **±30–40%** | Independent uncertainties combined |

For policy purposes, a **±25% band** is recommended as a practical working range. The theoretical composite of ±30–40% represents worst-case compounding; in practice, the convergence between supply-side and demand-side analyses provides a cross-check that substantially reduces effective uncertainty. When two independent methods produce overlapping ranges, confidence in the central estimate is higher than either method alone would support.

For implementation, this translates to multiplier ranges applied to the central parcel-level estimate:

| Application | Multiplier Range | Use Case |
|---|---|---|
| Standard | 0.85x – 1.15x | Typical parcels with good assessment data |
| Extended | 0.75x – 1.25x | Unusual parcels or thin assessment data |
| Administrative bounds | 0.70x – 1.30x | Policy floor and ceiling |

Arlington's median assessment ratio of 93.57% (2023 Virginia Assessment/Sales Ratio Study) means raw assessments should be multiplied by approximately **1.07** (1/0.9357) before applying uncertainty bands, to correct for systematic underassessment.

## Reconciling supply-side and demand-side frameworks: a worked example

The preceding sections develop two independent pricing frameworks — one derived from what sending-zone development rights are worth based on empirical data (supply side), the other from what receiving-zone developers would pay based on the economics of the alternative density pathway (demand side). This section traces representative parcels and projects through both frameworks to test whether they produce consistent dollar values. A 1:1 conversion ratio is assumed throughout: one square foot of severed residential capacity yields one square foot of bonus density at the receiving site.

### Sending-zone analysis: three representative parcels

The supply-side formula from the valuation framework is:

**TDR Value per Buildable SF = (Assessed Land Value × Discount Ratio) ÷ Maximum Buildable GFA**

This produces a per-square-foot value that applies uniformly across the parcel's total buildable capacity. Only the *unused* portion (maximum GFA minus current GFA) is available for transfer. The three parcels below span the range of R-district lot sizes, using conservative assumptions (no front porch bonus, 2.5 assumed stories within the 35-foot height limit).

### Supply-side price consistency across districts

The per-buildable-square-foot values are remarkably stable across all three districts and lot sizes: **$51–$72/sf** at the 0.55–0.75 discount range, with a midpoint of approximately **$60/sf**. This consistency arises because the ratio of assessed land value to maximum buildable GFA varies within a narrow band across Arlington's residential districts — larger lots have both higher land values and more buildable capacity. The stability is analytically useful: it means the discount parameters produce predictable, non-erratic credit prices across the sending-zone inventory.

| **Per SF of bonus density** | **$121.83–$193.25** | $6.14M–$9.74M ÷ 50,400 sf |

### The convergence test

At a 1:1 conversion ratio, the developer purchasing 50,400 sf of bonus density via TDR credits at sending-zone prices would pay:

| Discount Ratio | Credit Price/SF | Total TDR Cost (50,400 sf) | Site Plan Cost | TDR Savings |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 0.55 (low) | ~$51/sf | $2,571,000 | $6.14–9.74M | $3.57–7.17M |
| 0.65 (mid) | ~$61/sf | $3,074,000 | $6.14–9.74M | $3.07–6.67M |
| 0.75 (high) | ~$70/sf | $3,528,000 | $6.14–9.74M | $2.61–6.21M |

**The two frameworks converge with substantial margin.** At every point in the 0.55–0.75 discount range, the supply-side TDR credit cost ($2.57M–$3.53M) falls well below the demand-side ceiling ($6.14M–$9.74M). The gap is not marginal — TDR credits cost roughly **one-third to one-half** of the site plan alternative. This means:

1. **The discount parameters are economically viable.** A rational developer would strongly prefer TDR purchase over the site plan process at these prices.

2. **The parameters are not set at the demand-side maximum.** There is substantial headroom between the supply-side price and the demand-side ceiling. This headroom represents economic surplus that would be shared between sending-zone sellers (who receive more than the minimum they'd accept) and receiving-zone buyers (who pay less than the maximum they'd tolerate). The existence of this surplus is a sign of a viable market, not a pricing error.

3. **The parameters survive even if demand-side estimates are substantially wrong.** Even if actual site plan costs are only half the estimated range (~$60–$97/sf of bonus density), TDR credits at $51–$70/sf would still be competitive with or cheaper than the alternative.

4. **The gap also absorbs transaction costs.** Real-world TDR transactions involve brokerage, legal, and recording costs that reduce the net economic advantage. The $2.6M–$6.2M savings margin is wide enough to accommodate substantial transaction friction and still leave both parties better off.

### Sensitivity: AHIF contributions layered on TDR

A likely program design feature would require AHIF contributions alongside TDR credit costs, preserving the affordable housing funding pipeline. Even in that scenario, TDR remains economically attractive:

| Route | AHIF | TDR Credits | Other Site Plan Costs | Total | Per SF Bonus |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Site plan only | $1.44M | — | $4.70–8.30M | $6.14–9.74M | $121.83–193.25 |
| TDR + AHIF | $1.44M | $2.57–3.53M | — | $4.01–4.97M | $79.56–98.61 |
| **Developer savings with TDR + AHIF** | | | | **$1.17–5.73M** | **$23.21–$113.69** |

Even with AHIF contributions preserved, TDR saves the developer $1.2M–$5.7M — primarily by eliminating 12–18 months of carrying costs and removing the uncertainty premium associated with discretionary County Board approval.

### Structural findings from the reconciliation

**The parameters could be higher and still produce viable TDR pricing.** At discount = 0.75 (the ceiling), TDR credits cost ~$70/sf versus a site plan cost of ~$122–193/sf. The 0.75 upper bound is constrained by supply-side evidence (conservation easement ratios, tax court precedent), not by demand-side affordability.

**The parameters produce per-parcel credit values of $152,000–$329,000 for typical sending-zone lots.** These concrete dollar amounts provide the basis for evaluating program participation incentives in the program design context.

**The price-per-buildable-square-foot stability (~$51–$72/sf across districts) simplifies implementation.** Because the per-sf value varies within a narrow band regardless of lot size or district, the program could potentially use a flat per-sf price within each neighborhood market area rather than requiring parcel-level calculations — reducing administrative complexity while maintaining reasonable accuracy.

## Recommended parameters

### Primary recommendation

| Parameter | Value | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| **discount_low** | **0.55** | Lower bound of development rights as a fraction of assessed land value. Anchored by: (a) the demand-side floor — below this level, transferred rights represent too small a fraction of receiving-zone value for economic viability; (b) the lower range of conservation easement data adjusted downward for partial rather than total rights severance; (c) consistency with the lower end of operating TDR programs with comparable structures |
| **discount_high** | **0.75** | Upper bound. Anchored by: (a) the demand-side ceiling — above this level, TDR credits would approach or exceed the site plan alternative for some receiving-zone projects; (b) Maryland MALPF statutory cap of 75% of appraised value; (c) the upper range of tax court-upheld ratios for genuine development restrictions; (d) conservation easement data (60–80%) representing total rights elimination as a ceiling |

### Sensitivity to program design choices

The appropriate position within the 0.55–0.75 range depends on program design decisions:

**Conversion ratio**: How many square feet of severed residential capacity translate to one square foot of commercial bonus density? A 1:1 ratio means the development right has the same nominal area in both zones. A 2:1 ratio (two residential sf = one commercial sf) effectively halves the per-credit price for receiving-zone developers while doubling the number of credits needed. The conversion ratio is the primary lever for calibrating supply-side pricing to demand-side willingness-to-pay. The worked example in this report assumes 1:1.

**Mandatory vs. voluntary purchase**: If TDR purchase is the only pathway to bonus density (as in San Francisco), prices can be set higher because developers have no alternative. If TDR competes with the site plan process (as in Arlington's current structure), prices must be set below the all-in cost of site plan approval to attract buyers. This report's demand-side analysis assumes the latter, more conservative scenario.

**TDR bank**: A public TDR bank that buys and sells credits at administratively set prices (as in New Jersey's Pinelands) stabilizes the market and reduces transaction costs. Without a bank, bilateral negotiations between individual homeowners and commercial developers create friction that may depress realized prices below theoretical values.

The most significant finding of this analysis is the data gap in the final row. No large-scale TDR program operates in a context directly comparable to Arlington's — a dense, high-value inner suburb transferring unused residential capacity to commercial corridors. The closest parallels (NYC, San Francisco) involve much larger absolute values and different market dynamics. The recommended parameters bridge this gap using converging evidence from multiple independent approaches. Commissioned MAI appraisals on representative Arlington R-zoned parcels would generate Arlington-specific ratios that could further refine the 0.55–0.75 range.
